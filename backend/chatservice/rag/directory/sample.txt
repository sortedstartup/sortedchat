### 1. Early Resistance and Pre-Colonial Legacy

1. India’s history of resistance dates back to ancient times, with numerous kingdoms and empires defending their sovereignty.
2. The Maurya and Gupta empires exemplified strong centralized governance and military prowess.
3. The Mughal Empire, established in the 16th century, unified large parts of India under a centralized administration.
4. The Maratha Empire, emerging in the 17th century, was a significant force against Mughal dominance.
5. The Sikh Empire in the early 19th century, under Maharaja Ranjit Singh, was a formidable power in the northwest.
6. The British East India Company began its operations in India in the early 17th century.
7. The Battle of Plassey in 1757 marked the beginning of British political control in India.
8. The British gradually expanded their influence through diplomacy and military conquest.
9. The Indian subcontinent was divided into numerous princely states and territories.
10. The British implemented policies that centralized power and resources in their hands.

### 2. First War of Independence (1857–1858)

11. The 1857 rebellion, also known as the Sepoy Mutiny, was a significant uprising against British rule.
12. It began with the mutiny of Indian soldiers (sepoys) in the British East India Company’s army.
13. The rebellion spread across northern and central India, involving both soldiers and civilians.
14. Key leaders included Mangal Pandey, Rani Lakshmibai, and Bahadur Shah Zafar.
15. The British suppressed the rebellion with significant force, leading to widespread destruction.
16. The rebellion led to the dissolution of the British East India Company.
17. The British government took direct control of India, initiating the British Raj.
18. The rebellion is considered the first major expression of resistance against colonial rule.
19. It laid the foundation for future nationalist movements in India.
20. The British implemented policies to prevent such uprisings in the future.

### 3. Formation of Early Nationalist Movements

21. The Indian National Congress (INC) was founded in 1885 by Allan Octavian Hume.
22. Initially, the INC aimed to promote dialogue between the British government and educated Indians.
23. Leaders like Dadabhai Naoroji and Gopal Krishna Gokhale advocated for reforms within the British system.
24. The INC gradually shifted towards demanding greater self-governance for Indians.
25. The partition of Bengal in 1905 by Lord Curzon sparked widespread protests.
26. The Swadeshi Movement emerged as a response, promoting the use of Indian-made goods.
27. Leaders like Bal Gangadhar Tilak and Bipin Chandra Pal played pivotal roles in the movement.
28. The movement marked a significant shift towards mass participation in the freedom struggle.
29. The British reversed the partition in 1911 due to the movement's success.
30. The reversal was seen as a victory for Indian nationalism.

### 4. Rise of Mahatma Gandhi and Non-Violent Resistance

31. Mahatma Gandhi returned to India from South Africa in 1915.
32. He introduced the concept of Satyagraha, emphasizing non-violent resistance.
33. The Champaran Satyagraha in 1917 was Gandhi's first major movement in India.
34. It addressed the plight of indigo farmers under oppressive conditions.
35. The success of Champaran Satyagraha inspired similar movements across India.
36. The Non-Cooperation Movement was launched in 1920, urging Indians to withdraw from British institutions.
37. The movement gained mass support, with widespread boycotts of British goods and services.
38. The Chauri Chaura incident in 1922, where protesters killed policemen, led Gandhi to call off the movement.
39. The incident highlighted the challenges of maintaining non-violence during mass movements.
40. Despite the setback, Gandhi's philosophy of non-violence became central to the freedom struggle.

### 5. Civil Disobedience and Salt March

41. The Salt March in 1930 was a pivotal event in the Indian freedom movement.
42. Gandhi led a 240-mile march to produce salt from the sea, defying British laws.
43. The march attracted international attention to India's struggle for independence.
44. The British responded with mass arrests, including that of Gandhi.
45. The Salt March inspired widespread civil disobedience campaigns across India.
46. The movement highlighted the oppressive nature of British colonial laws.
47. It demonstrated the power of non-violent resistance in challenging colonial rule.
48. The Salt March remains a symbol of India's fight for freedom.
49. It also marked a significant shift towards mass mobilization in the independence movement.
50. The British government was forced to reconsider its policies in India due to the movement's impact.

### 6. Quit India Movement (1942)

51. The Quit India Movement was launched by Gandhi in 1942 during World War II.
52. Gandhi called for an immediate end to British rule in India.
53. The movement was a response to the British government's failure to grant India self-governance.
54. The British arrested Gandhi and other INC leaders, leading to widespread protests.
55. The movement was marked by mass arrests and repression by the British authorities.
56. Despite its suppression, the movement demonstrated the unyielding desire for independence.
57. It galvanized public opinion against British rule.
58. The movement's slogan, "Do or Die," became a rallying cry for freedom.
59. The Quit India Movement marked a turning point in India's struggle for independence.
60. It led to increased international pressure on Britain to decolonize India.

### 7. Role of Other Leaders and Movements

61. Subhas Chandra Bose was a prominent leader who advocated for armed resistance against British rule.
62. He formed the Azad Hind Fauj (Indian National Army) to fight for India's independence.
63. Bose's efforts garnered support from Indian expatriates and Axis powers during World War II.
64. Jawaharlal Nehru emerged as a key leader of the INC and a proponent of socialism.
65. Nehru's vision for India included industrialization and modernization.
66. Sardar Vallabhbhai Patel played a crucial role in integrating princely states into India post-independence.
67. Dr. B.R. Ambedkar championed the rights of Dalits and was the principal architect of the Indian Constitution.
68. The Khilafat Movement, led by Maulana Mohammad Ali and Shaukat Ali, sought to protect the Ottoman Caliphate.
69. The movement gained support from Muslims across India and aligned with the Non-Cooperation Movement.
70. The role of women in the freedom struggle was significant, with leaders like Sarojini Naidu and Kasturba Gandhi actively participating.
